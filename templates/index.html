Introduction

The portfolio analysis provides an intuitive understanding of how given a set of factors affect the components and the future behavior.  Such analysis would be beneficial mostly to the smaller portfolios or individual investors.

 

The choice of factors is really important! Traditionally, similar analyses use an index as a primary factor, but diverse macro-economic factors such as interest rate, GDP, currencies, unemployment rate might provide better insights.

 

 

Theory

A portfolio can be comprised of any number of stocks over 0.5 – 3 years period. The factors are variables that reflect overall economy and affect the portfolio

 

For the correlation analysis, the daily returns of each individual component is linearly regressed against daily returns of each factor, and the regression coefficients are used to predict changes in the portfolio. 

 

The regression is computed using the daily returns of the time series for either components or factors because the returns time series is stationary (constant variance and mean) while the absolute price is not.  Regression over non-stationary series gives unstable results. 

 

Each component $y_i$ is assumed linearly related to $x_i$s:

$y_i=\alpha_i+\beta _1*x_{i1}+…+\beta_p*x_{ip}$

 

$\alpha$ is the y-intercept, which is the part that cannot be explained by the factor, and usually indicates idiosyncratic risk. For this analysis, alpha is computed by adding a constant term to the regression.

 

$\beta$ is the slope of the line that best fits the data against the factor. It is a historical measure of the stock's volatilities against the factor. For S&P500 is a good measure of the market as a whole. When looking at S&P500, the return generated from a portfolio can be attributed to overall market returns. The value $\beta$ can be interpreted for these conditions:

o   $\beta$ = 1, component moves with market

o   $\beta$ > 1, component is more volatile than the market (high tech)

o   $\beta$ < 1, component less volatile than the market (utility companies)

o   $\beta$ < 0, component is inverse to the market (gold)

o   $\beta$ = 0 component doesn't change with the market (cash)

 

There are two caveats in prediction using $\beta$

o   $\beta$ is a measurement of systematic risk, which is the risk that the market as a whole. It is based on the effect of market wide risk, not the risk of the company

o   $\beta$ is a historical measure of stock's volatility. This means that past volatility doesn't necessary predict future beta or future volatility. Study of Gene Fama and Ken French on cross-section of expected stock returns that examines the reliability of past $\beta$ indicates that individual stock’s past $\beta$ is not a good predictor of future $\beta$. $\beta$ tends to revert back to 1.

Efficient Frontier

Efficient frontier is a concept from modern portfolio theory introduced by Harry Markowitz. It shows the ideal optimal portfolio allocation with highest expected return based on the level of tolerated risks. This analysis shows the benefit of portfolio diversification, by trading off expected returns with tolerated risk.

 

For portfolio with no risk-free asset, the objective of EF is to minimize the difference between the variance of portfolio return and the expected return, while subject to the constraint that sum w is 1:

 

min $w^T\Sigma$$w=q*R^Tw$

w is a vector of portfolio allocation, and sum(w) = 1

$\Sigma$ is the covariance matrix for portfolio components

q >= 0 is the risk tolerance level

R is the expected return vector

Points of Interest based on Principal Components Analysis

One way to determine points of interest in the portfolio's past performance is by doing principal component analysis, where the analysis can be obtained through singular value decomposition. Though the physical interpretation of what is doing on during the points of interest is hard to extract.

 

To obtain the PCA, a vector X can be transformed into Y by multiplying a matrix P, where the columns of P are the eigenvectors of $X^TX$, such that the covariance of Y is diagonalized.

$PX = Y$

$X = U\Sigma$$V^T$

$X^TX = U\Sigma^2V^T$


The 1-PC is the eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalues, which might have the most significant contribution to the extract potential relationship between the portfolio components and a "latent" factor. In this interpretation, it is possible to look at the ratio of the negative values in the corresponding first eigenvector. The anxiety detection trigger can be set to such when the ratio of the negative values in the corresponding eigenvector increases consecutively over 5 days. These points of interest are plotted in red dots. They can be used to signal non-financial events' effect on the market.
